# Conclusion: Legacy

The 4 Per Cent Loan of 1907 was redeemed in July 1907. It was the last bond in this book's sweep, and in many ways the last bond of the long 19th century of American federal debt management. Within months, the Panic of 1907 was underway. Within six years, the Federal Reserve System was in place. Within ten, the United States was selling Liberty Loans to twenty-two million subscribers to fight a war that would consume more federal cash than every previous war combined. The world that Hamilton, Gallatin, McCulloch, and Sherman had built — sinking funds, par-value placements, bank-mediated public distributions, semi-annual coin coupons — was passing into a different world.

This concluding essay tries to take stock. What did the 26 bonds in this book together produce? What did the institutional architecture they trace ultimately bequeath? And how do we read Bayley today, in light of what came after him?

## Three institutional bequests

### 1. The principle of par-value placement

The most important institutional bequest of the Hamilton-Gallatin era was a norm: that federal bonds should be sold at par. This was not an obvious choice. Most European sovereigns of the late 18th century routinely placed debt at discount; many of the underwriting houses of London, Amsterdam, and Paris organized themselves around the discount-placement model. Hamilton's 1790 commitment to par placement was a signaling choice — a way of telling the world that the United States would not behave like a typical sovereign.

That commitment held, with a few notable exceptions, for over a century. The exceptions are themselves instructive. The Sixteen Million Loan of 1813 was issued at \$88 (a 12% discount) because the financial machinery required for par placement had been allowed to lapse with the expiration of the First Bank in 1811. The Treasury Notes of 1860 were issued at 9% — a discount embedded in the coupon rather than the principal — because the secession crisis had broken federal credit. Both were exceptions that proved the rule, and in both cases the response was institutional reform: the Second Bank of the United States in 1816, and the Legal Tender Act / National Banking Act / Five-Twenty marketing system in 1862–1863.

By the time the 4% Loan of 1907 was issued, this principle was so firmly established that nobody noticed. American federal bonds were placed at par. They could trade above par in the secondary market — and the 4% Loan did so for most of its 30-year life — but they were sold at par. The Liberty Loans of 1917–1919 inherited this norm without question.

### 2. The principle of mass public ownership

The second bequest was the idea that federal debt belongs, in significant part, to the public — not just to banks and wealthy investors. This idea is so familiar today that we forget how recent it is. Before 1862, federal bonds were the province of a few hundred merchants and a handful of banks, plus European underwriters. The Six Per Cent Stock of 1790 had perhaps 14,326 holders by 1803 (per Bayley's own count). The Loan of 1848 was placed primarily through New York and Philadelphia merchant houses.

It was **Jay Cooke and the Five-Twenties of 1862** that broke this pattern. Cooke's 2,500-salesman national distribution network sold federal bonds to four million Americans, in denominations as small as $50 — accessible, just barely, to a working-class household. Bayley narrates Cooke's achievement with characteristic Treasury-Department understatement:

> The history of the world may be searched in vain for a parallel case of popular financial support to a national government.[^chase-bayley]

[^chase-bayley]: Salmon P. Chase, quoted in Bayley, *National Loans*, ch. 4 (Five-Twenties of 1862), citing Chase's annual report to Congress, December 10, 1863.

The mass-marketing template Cooke built became the explicit model for the **Liberty Loans of 1917–1919**, which sold roughly $21.5 billion in bonds to over 22 million subscribers. The further mass-bond campaigns of WWII — the U.S. War Bond program of 1941–1945 — sold $185.7 billion to 85 million Americans. The principle that federal debt is owned by the citizens, not just by Wall Street, has shaped both American politics and American macroeconomics for over a century. Its origin is here, in the 1862 Five-Twenty.

### 3. The principle of refunding

The third bequest was the idea that high-coupon debt issued during emergencies can and should be refinanced into lower-coupon debt during peace. This was the explicit logic of the **Funding Act of August 4, 1790** — Hamilton's original — and it is the explicit logic of the **Funding Act of July 14, 1870** — Sherman's recurrence. Between them, these two acts constitute the bookends of the American refunding tradition.

Each followed a war. Each consolidated a chaotic mass of older obligations into a small number of standardized, lower-coupon, longer-dated instruments. Each saved the Treasury significant interest cost — Hamilton's mechanism via the Three Per Cent Stock and the Deferred Six Per Cent Stock, Sherman's via the explicit refunding-bond ladder of the 5%, 4.5%, and 4% Loans. The 4% Loan of 1907 alone saved the Treasury approximately \$600 million in interest over its 30-year life relative to what the Civil War 6% debt would have cost.

The refunding template would be used again, repeatedly. The Liberty Loan refundings of 1922–1923 (when Treasury Secretary Andrew Mellon refinanced wartime 4.25% debt at 4% and below). The post-WWII conversion of high-coupon war bonds into lower-coupon Treasuries. The modern Treasury practice of "refunding auctions" every quarter, where maturing securities are rolled over into new ones. All of it descends, in spirit and in form, from the 1790 and 1870 Funding Acts.

## Two interpretive themes

### The credibility ratchet

If you read the price histories of all 26 bonds in sequence, what stands out is not any single price level. It is the **systematic improvement in price stability and trading premium** across the long sweep. The Six Per Cent Stock of 1790 trades from $70 (1790) to $128.75 (1792) — a range of more than 80% — within its first two years. The 4% Loan of 1907, by contrast, trades within a much narrower band — peak $129.52, trough $98.88, mean $114.11 — across its entire 30-year life.

This is the **credibility ratchet** of American sovereign debt. Each successful crisis-and-recovery cycle — Hamilton's stabilization in 1792, Gallatin's discipline through the early 1810s, the post-Civil War recovery, the resumption of specie payments in 1879, the gold standard's restoration in 1900 — added a small additional layer of confidence that markets could henceforth take for granted. By 1907, U.S. sovereign debt was a low-volatility instrument. By 1917, it could absorb $21.5 billion of war financing without breaking. By 1945, it could absorb $185.7 billion. The ratchet had become an avalanche of credibility.

### The institutional fragility

But the credibility ratchet was not automatic. At several points in this book's narrative, we see how quickly it could go in reverse. The Treasury Notes of 1860 are the clearest case: in less than a year, federal credit went from solid (the Loan of 1848 trading near $130 in late 1859) to broken (Treasury Notes at 9%, the Loan of 1848 at $88 in August 1861). The reversal was political. The recovery, when it came, required institutional reconstruction — Legal Tender Act, National Banking Act, Five-Twenty marketing system — on an unprecedented scale.

The lesson, written across these 26 bonds, is that **sovereign credit is an institutional achievement, not a natural endowment**. It requires central-banking infrastructure (or its functional equivalent), it requires a fiscal commitment to honor obligations even at political cost, and it requires market structures capable of absorbing borrowing in scale. When any of these fail, credit fails with them — quickly. The Treasury Notes of 1860 are nine months of evidence on that point. The Sixteen Million Loan of 1813 are another two years.

The modern reader, looking at U.S. Treasury debt today and forgetting how dearly bought its current AAA rating was, will find the second half of this book sobering. The credibility we now take for granted was constructed bond by bond, statute by statute, crisis by crisis, across the 144 years between Hamilton's first emergency loan and the redemption of the 4% Loan of 1907. It can be dismantled in a fraction of that time, if the institutional architecture that supports it is allowed to lapse.

## Where the story goes after 1907

This book ends in 1907 because the bonds in it end in 1907. But the story of American sovereign debt does not end there, and a brief sketch of what follows may help the reader place the work in context.

The **Federal Reserve Act of 1913** centralized monetary policy in a single institution, replacing the loose network of private New York banks that had served as fiscal agents to the Treasury since the demise of the Second Bank in 1836. The Fed's relationship with the Treasury would become the single most important institutional fact about American debt management for the rest of the 20th century.

The **Liberty Loans of 1917–1919** scaled the mass-marketed bond model to dimensions that Cooke could not have imagined: $21.5 billion across five issues, with one in five Americans subscribing. The Liberty Loan playbook was an explicit refinement of the 1862 Five-Twenty playbook; Treasury Secretary William Gibbs McAdoo studied Cooke's 1862 campaign carefully before designing his own.

The **Treasury Bill** was introduced in 1929, providing the Treasury with a true money-market instrument — a discount-issued, non-interest-bearing short-term obligation — to replace the cumbersome Treasury Notes of the 19th century. The bill was a direct descendant of the One Year Notes of 1863 (Bond ID 20105) and the various Certificates of Indebtedness of the Civil War era.

The **WWII bond program (1941–1945)** sold \$185.7 billion in bonds to 85 million Americans. By the war's end, the federal debt-to-GDP ratio reached approximately 120% — the highest in American history before or since. The infrastructure that made this possible — the Federal Reserve, the income tax, the mass-bond marketing apparatus, the Hall-Payne-Sargent-style data systems, the standardized auction procedures — was all, in one way or another, built on the foundations laid in the 1789–1907 period this book covers.

When you read modern U.S. Treasury operations — auction announcements, refunding statements, debt-management reports — you are reading documents whose conceptual framework was set down by Hamilton in 1790, refined by Gallatin in 1801–1814, transformed by Chase in 1862–1864, refined by Sherman in 1877–1881, and tabulated by Bayley himself in 1881. The 4% Loan of 1907's redemption was the closing of a chapter, not the closing of the book.

## A final word on Bayley

Rafael A. Bayley wrote, in his August 1881 letter of transmittal:

> I trust that the information furnished in these sheets may be promotive of a more general knowledge of our fiscal history, and more especially of the early financial struggles of our government, and that, as a work of reference, the compilation may be useful to many persons in official life.[^bayley-final]

[^bayley-final]: Bayley, *National Loans*, frontmatter, letter of transmittal, August 1, 1881.

A century and a half later, the answer to his hope is yes. His work has remained the foundation on which all subsequent American public-debt history has been built. The Hall-Payne-Sargent database has, at last, given us the secondary-market companion he could not have produced. This book is one attempt to read both together, in the hope that — as Bayley put it — the compilation "may be useful to many persons" beyond the official lives of the U.S. Treasury Department, in 1881 or in any subsequent year.

The story of American sovereign credit is not finished. The 4% Loan of 1907 closed a chapter; it did not close the book. But the foundations it documents — the institutional, market-structural, and credibility achievements — remain the load-bearing walls of every U.S. Treasury operation conducted in the 117 years since its redemption. We end where Bayley ended, with gratitude for what those before us built, and with the obligation to maintain it.
