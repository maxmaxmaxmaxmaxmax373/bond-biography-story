# The Grand Arc, 1789–1907

The story this book tells, in 26 bonds, runs from the moment Alexander Hamilton borrowed his first $100,000 from the Bank of North America in September 1789 to the moment Treasury Secretary George Cortelyou retired the last $30 million of the 4% Loan of 1907, in the summer of that same year, just as the Panic of 1907 was beginning. Between those two dates, the United States moved from a credit-doubtful new republic without a functioning treasury into one of the most reliably-borrowing sovereign nations on earth. The story has natural acts. Bayley wrote much of the script.

## I. The Hamilton Construction (1789–1801)

Bayley opens his account of the early federal debt with characteristic Treasury-Department understatement. The new government, he wrote, faced

> a mass of debt which devolved upon the United States, as an inheritance from the Continental Congress and the several colonies. … The debt was very large and depreciated, and was still depreciating in value; but it was the price of liberty, and the payment of that portion of it contracted by national authority was made obligatory by the new Constitution.[^bayley-debt-mass]

[^bayley-debt-mass]: Bayley, *National Loans*, ch. 2 ("Six Per Cent., Three Per Cent., and Deferred Six Per Cent. Stocks of 1790"), p. 83.

What Bayley calls "very large" was, in modern terms, on the order of $77 million in face value — at a moment when the entire federal budget for fiscal 1790 would be barely $1.7 million. Hamilton's solution, embodied in the **Funding Act of August 4, 1790**, was an act of financial engineering disguised as moral commitment. Holders of old Revolutionary debt would receive new federal bonds at full face value (the political imperative), but the new bonds would be split across **three Stocks** with carefully staggered cash flows (the fiscal imperative): the Six Per Cent Stock paying immediately, the Deferred Six Per Cent Stock waiting until 1801 before paying, and the Three Per Cent Stock at half the headline coupon. Hamilton's report to Congress framed it in moral language — "preserving public faith and integrity by fulfilling, as far as was practicable, the public engagements" — but the arithmetic was as careful as any modern restructuring deal.

The Hall–Payne–Sargent database tells us how the market responded. The Six Per Cent Stock opened at roughly $70 in November 1790, climbed to **$128.75 by January 1792** during the speculative boom around the chartering of the First Bank of the United States, then crashed during the Panic of 1792 — the first true financial crisis of the American republic — when Hamilton himself executed what is now recognized as the United States' first central-bank-style market-stabilizing intervention. Through the rest of the 1790s, the Stocks settled into a rhythm of trading slightly below par, with the Deferred Stock building a slow premium as the activation date approached and the Three Per Cent Stock sitting at the deep discounts that low-coupon perpetuals had traded at since the British Consols of 1751. By the end of the decade, the question was no longer *whether* American sovereign debt was creditworthy. It was a question of price and discipline.

## II. The Gallatin Discipline (1801–1812)

Hamilton's successor at Treasury, briefly Oliver Wolcott Jr. and then under Jefferson the Genevan-born **Albert Gallatin**, took the new credit Hamilton had built and committed to extinguishing the debt that backed it. Gallatin believed, with Jefferson, that excessive sovereign debt corrupted republican government. He set out to retire the entire federal debt, and over his thirteen-year tenure (1801–1814) he made remarkable progress: federal debt outstanding fell from \$83 million in 1801 to \$45 million by 1812, a reduction of nearly half across a single decade.

The bonds in this collection from the Gallatin years tell the story. The **Louisiana Six Per Cent Stock** of 1804 (Bond ID 20044) financed the Louisiana Purchase by the elegant device of issuing $11.25 million in bonds directly to France via the Hope & Baring banking syndicate of Amsterdam — the first major American sovereign debt placed primarily in international capital markets. The **Exchanged and Converted Six Per Cent Stocks of 1807** consolidated the older 1790s and early-1800s instruments into a standardized series, simplifying the Treasury's operational burden and clearing the way for systematic retirement.

It was a discipline that could not survive a real war. When James Madison declared war on Great Britain in June 1812, the institutional infrastructure Hamilton had built — most importantly the First Bank of the United States — had just been allowed to expire (February 1811), and federal credit was thrown back on the resources of private bankers in New York and Philadelphia. The story of the **Six Per Cent Loan of 1812**, the **Sixteen Million Loan of 1813**, and the **Seven and One-Half Million Loan of 1813** is the story of how three private financiers — Stephen Girard of Philadelphia, John Jacob Astor of New York, and David Parish of Hamburg — saved the United States war effort by absorbing the bulk of the war loans when public subscription failed. The Sixteen Million Loan was placed at $88 to par — a 12% discount, a sharp departure from the Hamiltonian par-value tradition. Senator Rufus King of New York remarked, in the heat of the moment, that

> Were it not for Mr. Girard, Mr. Astor, and Mr. Parish, the loan must have absolutely failed; and the Government been compelled to make peace on any terms, however dishonorable.[^king-quote]

[^king-quote]: Quoted in McMaster, J. B. (1918), *The Life and Times of Stephen Girard*, vol. 1, p. 234.

The lesson of 1812–1815 — that wartime borrowing required institutional infrastructure beyond ad-hoc private rescues — drove the chartering of the **Second Bank of the United States** in 1816. By 1820, with the war over, the worst of the postwar Panic of 1819 receding, and the Second Bank stabilizing the financial system, the Six Per Cent Loan of 1812 was trading at $109.50 — its lifetime peak. Sovereign credibility had survived its first stress test.

## III. The Antebellum Plateau (1820–1846)

The third act is largely off the page of this book. Between 1820 and 1846, the United States had little need for major new federal borrowing. Customs revenues from a booming economy proved sufficient to retire the War of 1812 debt at a steady clip, and by **January 1835** Andrew Jackson formally announced what is still the only debt-free moment in American history: the federal debt had been fully extinguished.

The bonds we have included from this era — the Loan of 1846 and the Mexican Indemnity Stock of 1846 — sit at the inflection point where this long deflation gave way to renewed borrowing for the Mexican-American War. The Polk administration's Treasury Secretary **Robert J. Walker** financed that war on a hybrid model — moderate tariff revenues, short-term Treasury Notes, and one major long-term bond issue — and did so with relative ease, in part because of the Independent Treasury Act of 1846 which separated federal finances from the private banking system. The Mexican War cost approximately $75 million in direct expenditures; the Loan of 1846 raised only $5 million of that total. By the standards of 1813, this was elegant.

The **Loan of 1848** is the longest-lived bond in this collection's antebellum group. Issued at a 3% premium over par in March 1849 to finance both the final installments of the Mexican Cession payment and the early infrastructure of the post-1848 American state, it traded continuously from late 1848 through the Civil War's first years. Its 168 monthly price observations are one of the cleanest windows in financial history onto the **antebellum boom**: prices climbed steadily from $103 (1849) to $133.56 (December 1859) as California gold inflows, railroad expansion, and immigrant-fueled economic growth pushed up the value of high-grade U.S. Treasury debt. The brief Panic of 1857 dip is barely visible. The market, throughout the 1850s, was sanguine.

## IV. The Sectional Crash (1857–1861)

And then, in late 1860, it was not. Following Lincoln's election on November 6, 1860 and the resignation of Treasury Secretary Howell Cobb on December 8 (Cobb returned to Georgia to support secession), federal credit collapsed within weeks. The **Treasury Notes of 1860** — authorized by the Act of December 17, 1860, three days before South Carolina seceded — could only be placed at **9%**, the highest peacetime coupon ever paid on a U.S. federal bond. Bayley narrates this moment with a Treasury insider's residual outrage:

> The Government of the United States is now in a condition that demands the most prompt and decisive action.[^cobb-quote]

[^cobb-quote]: Cobb, H., outgoing Treasury Secretary, December 8, 1860, in his final letter to President Buchanan, quoted in Bayley, *National Loans*, ch. 3.

The Loan of 1848, still in the secondary market, fell from $133.56 (December 1859) to $88.00 (August 1861) — a 34% drop in twenty months. This is one of the rare occasions in pre-Federal-Reserve American financial history when the bond market clearly **anticipated political catastrophe** before it fully arrived. The decline began before South Carolina's secession; it accelerated through the Buchanan-Lincoln transition; it bottomed in the months immediately after Fort Sumter, when the war's outcome was genuinely uncertain.

## V. The Civil War Transformation (1861–1865)

The Civil War broke the antebellum framework of federal finance, and reconstructed it on a vastly larger scale. Bayley's chapter on the war begins with a remarkable understatement:

> The extra session of Congress which began July 4, 1861, was held under very different circumstances from those which had accompanied any preceding session. Eleven states had attempted to secede from the Union, and were in actual rebellion.[^bayley-civil-war-opening]

[^bayley-civil-war-opening]: Bayley, *National Loans*, ch. 4 ("Loan of July and August, 1861"), p. 23.

The financial story Bayley then tells, and that this book extends with monthly price data, is one of the most consequential reconstructions of a sovereign credit system in financial history. In the space of four years, the United States Treasury raised approximately $2.7 billion in debt — roughly forty times the entire pre-war federal budget. It did so through a tiered architecture of instruments designed to attract different categories of holder:

- The **Loan of July and August 1861** (Bond ID 20099) — a conventional 6% long bond, sold to banks and traditional creditors through traditional underwriting.
- The **Five-Twenties of 1862** (Bond ID 20101) — Jay Cooke's mass-marketed bond. Sold to four million ordinary Americans through a 2,500-salesman national distribution network. The first truly democratic federal bond.
- The **One Year Notes of 1863** (Bond ID 20105) — short-term liquidity paper, the ancestor of the modern Treasury bill.
- The **Ten-Forties of 1864** (Bond ID 20108) — Salmon P. Chase's experiment with a lower 5% coupon. It nearly failed; Chase resigned over its placement.
- The **Five-Twenties of June 1864** and **of 1865** — Fessenden's and McCulloch's reversion to the well-established 6% structure when the lower-coupon experiment proved premature.
- The **Consols of 1865** — McCulloch's debt-consolidation instrument, the largest single Treasury issue of the war.

These six instruments alone, viewed in price-and-quantity counterpoint, contain most of what is interesting about Civil War finance. The 1862 Five-Twenties' 154-month price history runs from a wartime low of $94.00 (February 1863, Cooke's nadir) to a postwar peak of $123.88 (July 1869, immediately after the Public Credit Act mandated coin redemption). Outstanding peaked at $514.8 million in September 1868. By 1877 the entire issue had been retired through the great refunding operations of the early 1870s.

## VI. The Sherman Refunding (1870–1907)

The book's third act closes with the **Funding Act of July 14, 1870** — Hamilton's 1790 Funding Act recurring, three generations later, to do for the Civil War what the original had done for the Revolution. Treasury Secretary **John Sherman** (brother of General William Tecumseh Sherman, who had taken Atlanta) used the 1870 Act to refinance roughly \$2 billion of 6% Civil War debt into new long-dated bonds at 5%, 4.5%, and 4% coupons. The annual interest savings were approximately \$40 million — equivalent, in 1880 dollars, to a substantial fraction of the entire federal civilian payroll.

Two of the three refunding bonds are biographized in this book: the **Four and One-Half Percent Loan of 1891** (Bond ID 20120) and the **Four Percent Loan of 1907** (Bond ID 20121). The 4% Loan of 1907 is the largest single bond issue in this collection — peak outstanding \$739.4 million — and the longest-priced, with 357 monthly observations spanning from March 1878 through final redemption in July 1907. Its mean price across that thirty-year life was \$114.11. Its peak — \$129.52 in May 1887 — coincided with the deepest deflation of the Gilded Age and the high-water mark of international demand for American long-dated gold-standard sovereign paper.

When the 4% Loan was retired in 1907, the Panic of 1907 was already underway. Within six years, the Federal Reserve System would be in place. The Hamilton-Gallatin-McCulloch-Sherman world of debt management — sinking funds, par-value placements, bank-mediated public placements — was passing into a world that the Liberty Loans of 1917, the Treasury bill of 1929, and the modern federal-debt management techniques of the 20th century would inhabit.

## What the bonds together show

Read in sequence, the 26 bonds in this book tell three connected stories.

First, they tell a **policy** story: the construction of the institutional architecture of American federal debt management — the Sinking Fund (1790), the First and Second Banks of the United States (1791, 1816), the Independent Treasury (1846), the National Banking System (1863), the Public Credit Act (1869), the Funding Act (1870), and ultimately the Federal Reserve (1913). Each bond is a moment in this institutional construction.

Second, they tell a **market** story: the maturation of American capital markets from the small bank-mediated underwriting of 1790 to the mass-public-distribution of the Civil War to the international gold-standard refunding of the 1880s. The price histories trace this maturation directly. A Six Per Cent Stock of 1790 trading at $70 in its opening month is a different financial object from a Four Percent Loan of 1907 trading at $129.52 in 1887 — and the institutional changes between them are visible in the data.

Third, and most importantly, they tell a **credibility** story: the slow, sometimes interrupted construction of the United States as a creditworthy sovereign borrower. The story is not monotonic. The Treasury Notes of 1860 at 9% are a genuine reversal. The 1815 trough of the Six Per Cent Loan of 1812 at $75 is another. But across the long sweep, the trend is unmistakable, and by 1907 it is essentially complete: the United States can borrow long-term at par at 4%, with deep international demand. This is the credibility that, more than any other single factor, would underwrite the financing of the two World Wars and the Cold War to come.

Bayley closed his 1881 book with a characteristic note of professional understatement, writing that the "responsibility for any errors that may exist in this work must rest entirely upon me." We can only echo him. The 26 biographies that follow are an attempt to honor his archival labor by extending it into the 21st century — to combine his statutory record with the modern data record so that, at last, the political and the financial stories can be read together.
