# Era Overview — Civil War & Refunding (1861–1907)

> The extra session of Congress which began July 4, 1861, was held under very different circumstances from those which had accompanied any preceding session. Eleven states had attempted to secede from the Union, and were in actual rebellion. Seventy-five thousand volunteers had been called into the field to suppress the unlawful combination, and Congress met in a military camp.[^bayley-1861]
>
> — Rafael A. Bayley, *National Loans of the United States* (1881), ch. 4.

[^bayley-1861]: Bayley, ch. 4 ("Loan of July and August, 1861"), p. 23.

So begins Bayley's chapter on the Civil War. Re-read it. The first sentence is conventional Treasury-Department prose; the second names eleven states attempting secession; the third evokes a Congress meeting "in a military camp". Then he proceeds to narrate, with characteristic restraint, what was in fact the largest sovereign borrowing program in human history up to that date — and the institutional reconstruction of American federal credit that the borrowing required.

The ten bonds biographized in this Part span 46 years: from the Loan of July and August 1861 (Bond ID 20099) through the Four Percent Loan of 1907 (Bond ID 20121). They are best understood not as ten separate stories but as **two halves of a single story**: the wartime construction of a vastly enlarged federal debt apparatus (1861–1865), and the postwar refunding that converted this expensive emergency borrowing into low-cost peacetime debt (1869–1907).

## The scale of what changed

The numbers are worth pausing over. Federal debt outstanding in March 1861, at Lincoln's inauguration, was approximately **\$65 million**. By August 1865, after the Treasury's largest wartime issuances, federal debt outstanding was approximately **\$2.7 billion** — a forty-fold increase in just over four years. By comparison, the entire War of 1812 had increased federal debt from approximately \$45 million (1812) to \$127 million (1816), a less-than-three-fold increase.

The Civil War financing was, in scale, of an entirely different order from anything the United States had previously attempted. It required institutional changes — new Treasury powers, new instruments, new marketing systems, new banking architecture — on a comparable scale. The bonds in this Part are the visible products of those changes.

## The four Treasury Secretaries

Three Treasury Secretaries supervised the wartime financing, and a fourth supervised the postwar refunding. Each shaped the bonds in this collection in distinctive ways.

### Salmon P. Chase (March 1861 – June 1864)

**Salmon P. Chase** of Ohio took office on March 7, 1861, with a Treasury that had just placed the punitive Treasury Notes of 1860 (Bond ID 20095) and had only \$2 million in cash. Within four years he supervised the placement of more than \$2 billion in war debt. His major instruments were:

- The **Loan of July and August 1861** (Bond ID 20099) — the first major Civil War long bond, conventional 6% coupon and 20-year term, placed primarily through New York banks.
- The **Five-Twenties of 1862** (Bond ID 20101) — Jay Cooke's mass-marketed bond. The most consequential single instrument of the war's financing.
- The **One Year Notes of 1863** (Bond ID 20105) — short-term liquidity paper, the ancestor of the modern Treasury bill.
- The **Ten-Forties of 1864** (Bond ID 20108) — Chase's experiment with a lower 5% coupon. It nearly failed; it was the proximate cause of his resignation.

Chase resigned on June 30, 1864, and was elevated to Chief Justice of the Supreme Court in December of that year. His tenure was the most consequential in the history of the Treasury between Hamilton's and Henry Morgenthau's. Bayley, writing seventeen years later, narrates Chase's decisions with a respect that occasionally rises to the level of admiration.

### William Pitt Fessenden (July 1864 – March 1865)

**William Pitt Fessenden** of Maine inherited the Treasury at the worst political moment of the Civil War, in the summer of 1864. Confederate forces had reached the outskirts of Washington (Jubal Early's raid). Lincoln was uncertain of re-election. The Ten-Forties had stalled. Fessenden's principal contribution was a return to the proven 6% structure for the **Five-Twenties of June 1864** (Bond ID 20110), which placed successfully and provided the financing that carried the war through to Atlanta's fall and Lincoln's re-election.

### Hugh McCulloch (March 1865 – March 1869)

**Hugh McCulloch** of Indiana took office in early March 1865 — five weeks before Appomattox — and supervised the war's closing financings:

- The **Five-Twenties of 1865** (Bond ID 20113), authorized just two days before Lee's surrender.
- The **Consols of 1865** (Bond ID 20114), the first of three Consol issues that consolidated older Civil War debt into standardized 6% instruments.

McCulloch was a hard-money advocate who immediately began retiring federal debt with budget surpluses. His program was politically controversial — Western farmers, who benefited from the inflation associated with greenback paper money, opposed his contractionary policies — but his successors continued it through the Funding Act period.

### John Sherman (March 1877 – March 1881)

**John Sherman** of Ohio (brother of General W. T. Sherman) supervised the great Funding Act refundings. Working under the **Funding Act of July 14, 1870** — which had passed during the Boutwell Treasury but became operationally significant under Sherman — he refinanced approximately \$2 billion of 6% Civil War debt into new bonds at 5%, 4.5%, and 4% coupons. The two refunding bonds in this collection both originated in his refunding program:

- The **Four and One-Half Percent Loan of 1891** (Bond ID 20120).
- The **Four Percent Loan of 1907** (Bond ID 20121) — the longest-lived and largest single issue in this collection.

Bayley, writing in 1881, was nearly contemporaneous with the late stages of Sherman's refunding work. His chapter on the Funded Loans of 1881, 1891, and 1907 is among the most up-to-the-minute portions of his book.

## The transformation in three lenses

What changed between the Loan of July and August 1861 (the first bond of Part III) and the Four Percent Loan of 1907 (the last)?

### Through a market-structure lens

The 1861 Loan was placed primarily through traditional underwriting — banks and wealthy investors absorbed it through New York and Philadelphia merchant houses. The 4% Loan of 1907 was placed through a sophisticated international syndicate (August Belmont & Co.; Drexel, Morgan & Co.) that distributed it to European investors as well as American institutions. Between these two endpoints, Jay Cooke's mass-marketing campaign for the 1862 Five-Twenties had introduced the idea of bonds-for-the-people, while the 1870 Funding Act had institutionalized refunding as a standing capacity rather than an emergency response.

### Through a coupon lens

The 1861 Loan paid 6% in coin. The 4% Loan of 1907 paid 4% in coin. Between these two endpoints, the federal government cut its long-bond coupon rate by one-third — and did so at issuance, on debt placed at par. This is the visible result of the credibility ratchet that had been operating since 1790, and that the Civil War's successful financing had advanced significantly.

### Through a price-stability lens

The 1862 Five-Twenties' price ranged from \$94.00 (early 1863) to \$123.88 (1869) — a 32% range. The 4% Loan of 1907's price ranged from \$98.88 (early 1879) to \$129.52 (1887) — a 31% range. The percentage range looks similar, but the *trajectory* was different: the 1862 bond's range was driven by **wartime stress** then **postwar political resolution** (the Public Credit Act); the 1907 bond's range was driven by **gold-standard restoration** then **deflation premium build**. The same numerical range corresponds to vastly different underlying market conditions, and the second is much closer to a modern low-volatility sovereign benchmark than the first.

## How to read Part III

The ten biographies that follow are the most data-rich in this book. Several of them — particularly the Five-Twenties of 1862 (154 monthly observations), the Loan of 1848 (still in trading from Part II), and the 4% Loan of 1907 (357 observations!) — have such substantial price-history records that they can be analyzed as standalone case studies in 19th-century sovereign-bond market behavior.

Readers approaching Part III in sequence will see the wartime stress (the bottom of the 1862 trough), the postwar resolution (the 1869 Public Credit Act), the refunding wave (the 4.5% and 4% Loans), and the long Gilded Age plateau (the deep premium of the 4% Loan in the 1880s). Readers interested in particular themes — Cooke's marketing, the gold-greenback debate, the Funding Act mechanics, the international placement of the late-19th-century refunding bonds — will find them concentrated in the relevant individual biographies.

After all ten biographies, the [Era Synthesis](99_era_synthesis.md) draws out the cross-bond patterns that the individual biographies make visible only piecemeal — particularly the parallels between the **Funding Act of August 4, 1790** (Hamilton's) and the **Funding Act of July 14, 1870** (Sherman's), which together bookend the long American refunding tradition.
