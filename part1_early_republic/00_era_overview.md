# Era Overview — The Early Republic (1789–1815)

> The mass of debt which devolved upon the United States, as an inheritance from the Continental Congress and the several colonies, engaged the attention of the national legislature soon after the adoption of the Constitution. The debt was very large and depreciated, and was still depreciating in value; but it was the price of liberty, and the payment of that portion of it contracted by national authority was made obligatory by the new Constitution.[^bayley-mass-debt]
>
> — Rafael A. Bayley, *National Loans of the United States* (1881), ch. 2.

[^bayley-mass-debt]: Bayley, ch. 2 ("Six Per Cent., Three Per Cent., and Deferred Six Per Cent. Stocks of 1790"), p. 83.

So Bayley introduces the founding period of American sovereign credit. It is a paragraph that bears re-reading. The debt was "the price of liberty" — Bayley is unusually rhetorical here, by his standards — but it was also, he immediately reminds us, "depreciating in value", and the new Constitution had made payment "obligatory". The achievement of the Early Republic was to convert this depreciating, doubtful, politically contested mass of obligation into the foundation of a creditworthy sovereign state. Eleven of the bonds biographized in this book document that conversion, from Hamilton's first $100,000 emergency loan in September 1789 to the Seven and One-Half Million Loan that helped finance the closing months of the War of 1812.

## The five problems Hamilton inherited

The fiscal situation Hamilton confronted upon taking office on **September 11, 1789** had five distinct components, only some of which were widely understood at the time:

1. **Foreign debt** of approximately \$11.7 million — primarily Dutch and French loans contracted during the Revolution. Most coupons were in default. Foreign creditors expected payment in coin (specie) and at the original contractual terms.

2. **Domestic federal debt** of approximately \$28.9 million in audited principal, plus another \$11.4 million in accrued unpaid interest. This was held primarily by Revolutionary War soldiers, suppliers, and a handful of wealthy speculators who had bought up other holders' depreciated certificates at $0.15 to $0.25 on the dollar.

3. **State debts** of approximately \$25 million — accumulated by the thirteen original states during the Revolution. Whether the federal government should *assume* these debts was the most politically charged question Hamilton faced.

4. **Continental currency and state paper money** — by 1790 essentially worthless. Hamilton chose, controversially, to honor this only at deep discounts, treating it as a separate problem from the audited debt.

5. **Operating cash** — essentially zero. The Treasury needed working capital before any of the longer-term restructuring could be designed and approved.

Bayley narrates these problems with characteristic care for institutional fidelity: he is conscious throughout that he is writing about decisions whose ramifications were still visible in 1881. His chapter on the **Funding Act of August 4, 1790** quotes Hamilton's reply to a confidential letter from Washington about the underlying philosophy:

> The general inducements to a provision for the public debt were:
>
> 1. To preserve the public faith and integrity by fulfilling, as far as was practicable, the public engagements.
> 2. To manifest a due respect for property, by satisfying the public obligations in the hands of the public creditors, and which were as much their property as their houses or their lands, their hats or their coats.
> 3. To revive and establish public credit, the palladium of public safety.
> 4. To preserve the government itself by showing it worthy of the confidence which was placed in it; to procure to the community the blessings which in innumerable ways attend confidence in the government, and to avoid the evils which in as many ways attend the want of confidence in it.[^hamilton-letter]

[^hamilton-letter]: Hamilton to Washington, July 29, 1792, quoted in Bayley, ch. 2, p. 87.

These four "inducements" — moral commitment to creditors, property-rights respect for bondholders, restoration of credit as a national-security asset, and the broader political legitimacy of the government — were the deep logic of everything that followed. Hamilton was explicit that public credit was a *political* good, not merely an economic one.

## The bonds in this section

The 11 biographies in Part I trace the construction Hamilton began and that Albert Gallatin extended. They are best understood in three sub-clusters.

### A. The Hamilton Construction (1789–1791) — 5 bonds

- [Temporary Loan of 1789](01_temporary_loan_1789.ipynb) (Bond ID 20019) — the first federal loan under the Constitution, $100,000 from the Bank of North America and the Bank of New York.
- [Temporary Loan of 1790](02_temporary_loan_1790.ipynb) (Bond ID 20020) — its statutorily-authorized successor.
- [Six Per Cent Stock of 1790](03_six_per_cent_stock_1790.ipynb) (Bond ID 20021) — the cornerstone of the funding system, paying 6% from issuance.
- [Deferred Six Per Cent Stock of 1790](04_deferred_six_per_cent_stock_1790.ipynb) (Bond ID 20022) — the deferred-coupon companion, paying 6% only after January 1, 1801.
- [Three Per Cent Stock of 1790](05_three_per_cent_stock_1790.ipynb) (Bond ID 20023) — the back-interest-capitalization instrument.

These five bonds document the most consequential debt restructuring in American history. By the time the 1790 Stocks were fully placed, the United States had moved from "credit-doubtful" to "credit-rated" in the global capital markets of London and Amsterdam. The Six Per Cent Stock in particular saw a dramatic price climb from $70 (November 1790) to $128.75 (January 1792) before crashing in the Panic of 1792 — the first true financial crisis of the new republic.

### B. The Gallatin Refinements (1804–1807) — 3 bonds

- [Louisiana Six Per Cent Stock](06_louisiana_six_per_cent_stock.ipynb) (Bond ID 20044) — the bond that financed the Louisiana Purchase, $11.25 million issued directly to France via the Hope & Baring banking syndicate.
- [Exchanged Six Per Cent Stock of 1807](07_exchanged_six_per_cent_stock_1807.ipynb) (Bond ID 20045) — the consolidation of older 1790s 6% obligations.
- [Converted Six Per Cent Stock of 1807](08_converted_six_per_cent_stock_1807.ipynb) (Bond ID 20046) — its sister consolidation instrument for fractional and non-6% conversions.

These three bonds document Gallatin's quieter work: rationalizing the older debt, opening international placement channels (the Louisiana Stock placed primarily in European markets), and making continued progress on debt reduction. By 1812 the federal debt had fallen from \$83 million (1801) to \$45 million.

### C. The War of 1812 Stress Test (1812–1813) — 3 bonds

- [Six Per Cent Loan of 1812](09_six_per_cent_loan_1812.ipynb) (Bond ID 20048) — the first major war bond, placed at par but absorbed only with substantial difficulty.
- [Sixteen Million Loan of 1813](10_sixteen_million_loan_1813.ipynb) (Bond ID 20052) — the largest single War of 1812 issue, placed at $88 (a 12% discount), saved by the personal commitment of Stephen Girard, John Jacob Astor, and David Parish.
- [Seven and One-Half Million Loan of 1813](11_seven_and_one_half_million_loan_1813.ipynb) (Bond ID 20054) — its August 1813 follow-on, placed at $88.25 under similar conditions.

These three bonds are the stress-test of the Hamilton-Gallatin system. They reveal what happens when wartime borrowing demands exceed the institutional capacity of the era — most importantly, when the First Bank of the United States has been allowed to expire (February 1811) and the Treasury must turn to private bankers for emergency financing. The lesson was absorbed: the Second Bank of the United States was chartered in 1816, and federal financial infrastructure was reconstructed for the postwar era.

## The arc, in price

The price histories in the database, taken together, tell a single story. The Six Per Cent Stock of 1790 — Hamilton's centerpiece — has a 382-month price record (November 1790 – December 1823), with mean $95.72 and a range from $70 (1790) to $128.75 (1792). The volatility in the early years was extraordinary. By the 1810s, however, the same bond was trading in a much narrower band, and by the early 1820s most of the 1790 Stocks were essentially at par or being retired.

The Six Per Cent Loan of 1812 traded from $75.00 (January 1815) to $109.50 (June 1821) across its decade. The 34-point range looks dramatic, but compare it to the 58-point range of the Six Per Cent Stock of 1790's first two years: the second time around, the United States survived a major war with much less price-volatility damage. The credibility ratchet was already operating.

By the time the Louisiana Six Per Cent Stock was being retired in 1819–1820 — at par — and the War of 1812 loans were being refinanced into the Five Per Cent Loan of 1820 and the Five Per Cent Loan of 1821 (lower coupons!), the United States had achieved what Hamilton had set out to achieve: a sovereign credit good enough to be borrowed against in peace at lower rates than war had cost. This is the bequest the Early Republic left to the Antebellum and Civil War eras that follow.

## How to read Part I

Each of the 11 biographies that follow opens with Bayley's framing — his statute citation, his narration of the political circumstances, the figures he tabulated. Modern paragraphs follow without break, extending what Bayley wrote toward what we now know that he could not have known: how the secondary market priced his bonds, what the volatility tells us in retrospect, how the bond connects to others in the family, and what its long downstream legacy turned out to be.

For readers approaching Part I cover-to-cover, the biographies are arranged in chronological order of issuance, but each is self-contained. The five 1790-cluster biographies are particularly tightly linked — readers may find it useful to read those five in sequence, since they were issued under a single statute and trade in counterpoint. The Gallatin and War-of-1812 clusters can be read more independently.

After all 11 biographies, the [Era Synthesis](99_era_synthesis.md) draws out the cross-bond patterns — par-value norm, sinking-fund discipline, debt-extinguishment trajectory — that the individual biographies make visible only piecemeal.
