# Era Synthesis — The Early Republic

Having read the 11 individual biographies, what do they tell us *together* that they could not tell us individually?

## Five cross-bond patterns

### 1. The par-value norm was a deliberate signal, not a default practice

Hamilton's choice to place the 1790 Stocks at par — in fact, to honor old depreciated debts at par face value — was a costly signal. It was costly in an immediate fiscal sense: the federal government was committing to interest payments larger than easy revenue could service. It was costly in a political sense: speculators who had bought continental certificates at \$0.15 on the dollar were being made whole at \$1.00, a transfer of wealth that James Madison and other anti-Federalists found indefensible. But the costliness was the point. By signaling commitment under conditions where defection was easy, Hamilton built credibility faster than incremental honest dealing could have.

The 1813 issuance of the Sixteen Million Loan at \$88 — a 12% discount — was a forced exception, and its **forcedness** is what made it salient. Bayley narrates it without editorial comment, but the implications were absorbed: when the Second Bank of the United States was chartered in 1816, the institutional architecture explicitly aimed at restoring par-value placement capacity. The Five Per Cent Loan of 1820, the Five Per Cent Loan of 1821, and the Four and One-Half Per Cent Loans of 1824 were all placed at par.

The norm survived the early 19th century with one major Civil War-era exception (occasional below-par placements in 1862–1864 when Cooke could not absorb them himself), and was firmly re-established by the Funding Act refundings of 1870–1881. By the time the 4% Loan of 1907 was placed at par, the norm was so naturalized that it required no defense.

### 2. The Sinking Fund was the fiscal hinge

The **Sinking Fund Commission** — created by the Funding Act of August 4, 1790, with Hamilton, Adams, Jay, Jefferson, and Randolph as initial members — was the institutional device that translated budget surpluses into debt retirement. Bayley refers to it throughout his narrative; modern readers may underestimate how unusual it was for a national government in 1790 to commit, by statute, to systematic debt retirement.

The Sinking Fund operated almost continuously from 1790 to 1835, and its work is visible in the **Total Outstanding** time series of every Hamilton-era bond in this collection. Each of the three 1790 Stocks shows the same characteristic shape: rapid issuance through the early-to-mid 1790s, a plateau, and then a long systematic decline accelerating after 1801 (when Gallatin took over Treasury). The Three Per Cent Stock peaked at \$19.09 million (December 1804) and fell to under \$1 million by 1820. The Six Per Cent Stock peaked at \$28.17 million (December 1794) and fell on a similar trajectory.

The end-state of this discipline was the **Jacksonian debt extinguishment of January 1835** — the only moment in U.S. history when federal debt has been zero. By that date, every bond in this Part I collection had been fully retired or stood at residual outstanding balances.

### 3. The Deferred Stock was a financial innovation worth its own footnote

The [Deferred Six Per Cent Stock of 1790](04_deferred_six_per_cent_stock_1790.ipynb) is, in retrospect, one of the most important pieces of financial engineering in American history, and it deserves more attention than it usually receives. By issuing a bond whose 6% coupon would not begin paying until January 1, 1801, Hamilton solved a fiscal-political dilemma that could have torpedoed the entire funding system. He honored par face value (the political imperative) while reducing the immediate cash flow burden by approximately one-third for the first decade (the fiscal imperative).

The Hall–Payne–Sargent database tells us how the market priced this. The Deferred Stock opened at approximately \$45 in late 1790 — implying a market discount rate of roughly 7% on a forward-starting 6% coupon stream. As the activation date approached, the price climbed: by 1800, just before activation, the Stock was trading near par. The price-time profile is as clean a natural experiment in inter-temporal bond pricing as exists in early-American financial data.

Variants of the deferred-coupon technique appear repeatedly in subsequent American debt management — most notably in the post-Civil War refunding bonds of the 1870s, where holders of older 6% obligations could exchange for new 4% or 4.5% bonds with deferred-redemption schedules. The lineage is direct.

### 4. The Three Per Cent Stock reveals a 42-year credibility climb

The [Three Per Cent Stock of 1790](05_three_per_cent_stock_1790.ipynb) has the longest price history of any bond in the Hall-Payne-Sargent database for this period: 488 monthly observations from December 1790 to November 1832. The price trajectory is one of the most analytically valuable in early-American financial history.

Because the 3% coupon was deeply below the prevailing market rate (typically 6%) for most of the bond's life, it traded at substantial discount throughout its early decades. Its lifetime trough — \$44.17 in June 1798, during the Quasi-War with France — corresponds to the deepest moment of perceived sovereign risk in the bond's history. From there, however, the price climbed essentially monotonically over the next 34 years to reach \$100.00 in September 1832 — the only time the bond ever touched par.

The climb is not a story about this single bond. It is a story about the **42-year improvement in American sovereign credit**. By 1832, the prospect of debt extinguishment had become so credible that even a 3% coupon bond was trading at par. This is the same credibility that, three years later, allowed the United States to extinguish its federal debt entirely under Jackson — and that, when borrowing resumed, made it possible to place new debt at lower coupons than ever before.

### 5. The 1812–1815 stress test was a near-miss, not a graceful recovery

It is tempting, in retrospect, to read the War of 1812 financing as a successful first wartime stress test of the new fiscal system. The price histories in the database paint a more cautionary picture. The Six Per Cent Loan of 1812 fell to \$75 in January 1815 — a 25% drop from par, and within weeks of the Hartford Convention's threat of New England secession. The Sixteen Million Loan of 1813 fell similarly. Federal credit was not destroyed, but it was severely impaired, and recovery required (a) the news of Treaty of Ghent, (b) the news of Andrew Jackson's victory at New Orleans, and (c) the chartering of the Second Bank of the United States — all within an 18-month window.

Without those three reinforcing recoveries, particularly the chartering of the Second Bank, the post-1815 trajectory could plausibly have been quite different. Indeed, the lessons of 1812–1815 — that wartime borrowing requires institutional infrastructure, that par-value placement is fragile under stress, that private financiers cannot indefinitely substitute for public financial architecture — would shape every subsequent debate about federal financial institutions, including the eventual chartering of the Federal Reserve System in 1913.

## What the era left behind

The Early Republic bequeathed to the rest of American fiscal history five durable institutions and norms:

1. **The federal funded debt as a recognized asset class** — held by individuals, banks, foreign investors, and (after 1816) the Second Bank of the United States.
2. **The par-value placement norm** — costly signaling, sustained as a credibility-building mechanism.
3. **The Sinking Fund discipline** — systematic debt retirement using budget surpluses, recurring across multiple administrations.
4. **The standardized refunding instrument** — perfected first in 1807 (Exchanged and Converted Stocks) and then again in 1870 (Funding Act bonds).
5. **The international-placement capability** — first demonstrated in 1804 with the Louisiana Stock's Hope & Baring distribution, and never abandoned thereafter.

When the Antebellum era opens with the Loan of 1846, the United States is not the credit-doubtful new republic of 1789. It is a credit-rated sovereign with five decades of compounding institutional reputation. The bonds in [Part II](../part2_antebellum/00_era_overview.md) inherit that reputation. The bonds in [Part III](../part3_civil_war_post/00_era_overview.md), surviving the secession crisis that briefly broke it, restore it on a vastly larger scale.

But that restoration is only possible *because* the Early Republic did the foundational work. Hamilton, Gallatin, the 1790 Sinking Fund Commissioners, and the New York banks that absorbed the early loans built something the rest of American fiscal history would inherit. These 11 biographies are an attempt to honor that work.
